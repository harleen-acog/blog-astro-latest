## The Problem With How Most People Learn

Most people learn by re-reading, highlighting, and reviewing their notes. It feels productive. It is largely ineffective.

These passive methods create familiarity, which the brain confuses with understanding. You recognise the material, so it feels like you know it — until you try to use it without the notes in front of you.

Effective learning feels harder, not easier. That difficulty is the signal that real learning is happening.

---

## The Principles That Actually Work

### Retrieval Practice

The single most effective learning technique is attempting to recall information from memory before looking it up.

Every time you retrieve something from memory, you strengthen that memory. Every time you re-read something, you are mostly recognising it, not retrieving it.

In practice, this means:

- Closing your notes and writing down everything you remember after a study session
- Using flashcards where you are forced to produce an answer before seeing it
- Testing yourself regularly rather than reviewing

The initial struggle of retrieval practice feels frustrating. That frustration is the point.

### Spaced Repetition

Reviewing material at increasing intervals over time is dramatically more effective than reviewing it intensively in a short period.

Cramming works for a test on Friday. It does not work for retaining information a month later.

A simple version of this: review new material after one day, then after three days, then after a week, then after two weeks. Each time you successfully recall something, the interval before the next review grows.

### Interleaving

Practising multiple related topics or skills in a mixed order, rather than mastering one before moving to the next, leads to better long-term retention.

It feels less efficient because you make more errors. But the errors force your brain to work harder to discriminate between concepts, which strengthens understanding.

### The Generation Effect

Generating an answer, even an incorrect one, before being told the correct answer leads to better memory of the correct answer.

This is counterintuitive — being wrong seems like it should be a setback. But the struggle to generate an answer primes the brain to absorb the correct information more deeply.

## The Role of Sleep in Learning

Memory consolidation happens primarily during sleep. Material studied before sleep is retained significantly better than material studied earlier in the day with hours of activity afterward.

This is not an argument for pulling all-nighters before an exam. It is an argument for studying something shortly before sleep and reviewing it again the following morning.

---

## Understanding Over Memorisation

Facts without understanding are fragile. They are easily forgotten and difficult to apply.

When learning something new, ask:

- Why is this true?
- How does it connect to what I already know?
- What would change if this were different?
- Can I explain it simply, without jargon?

If you cannot answer these questions, you have information. If you can, you are starting to have understanding.

---

## Applying This in Practice

You do not need a complex system. A few changes make a significant difference:

- After reading or watching something, close it and write down the key ideas from memory
- Review that material again 24 hours later
- When practising a skill, mix up the types of problems rather than drilling one type to completion
- Prioritise sleep before and after intensive learning

The gap between how people typically study and how effective learning actually works is large. Closing that gap is less about working harder and more about working differently.
