## Why Financial Literacy Matters

Most people were never taught how money actually works. School covers algebra and history, but not how to avoid debt, build savings, or think about the future.

The result is that millions of people earn decent incomes but still live paycheck to paycheck — not because they spend recklessly, but because nobody gave them a framework.

---

## The Foundation: Know Where Your Money Goes

Before you can improve anything, you need visibility.

Track every expense for one month. Not to judge yourself, but to see the reality clearly. Most people are surprised by at least one category.

A simple breakdown that works for many people is the 50/30/20 rule:

- 50% on needs — rent, food, utilities, transport
- 30% on wants — dining out, entertainment, hobbies
- 20% on saving and paying off debt

This is a starting point, not a rigid law. Adjust based on your situation.

---

## The Emergency Fund

Before investing or aggressively saving for goals, build an emergency fund.

The target is three to six months of living expenses, kept in a separate savings account that you do not touch for anything other than genuine emergencies.

This one buffer eliminates the need to go into debt when something unexpected happens — a medical bill, a car repair, a gap between jobs.

---

## Debt: The Order of Operations

Not all debt is equally urgent.

High-interest debt, like credit cards, compounds fast and should be paid off before most other financial moves. Low-interest debt, like a student loan at a reasonable rate, is less urgent.

A common approach:

- Pay minimums on everything
- Put extra money toward the highest interest debt first
- Once that is cleared, roll that payment into the next one

This is called the avalanche method and it minimises the total interest paid.

---

## Saving vs Investing

Saving is keeping money safe. Investing is putting money to work.

A savings account protects your money but grows it slowly. Investing carries risk but over long time horizons, it historically outpaces inflation significantly.

A simple starting point for investing:

- Contribute enough to your employer's retirement plan to get any matching contribution — that is free money
- After that, consider a low-cost index fund rather than trying to pick individual stocks

---

## The Biggest Lever: The Gap Between Income and Spending

Wealth is not about how much you earn. It is about the gap between what you earn and what you spend.

Someone earning a high salary who spends all of it is not building wealth. Someone earning an average salary who consistently saves 20% is.

Widening that gap — either by earning more, spending less, or both — is the core of every financial plan.

---

## Where to Start Today

If this feels overwhelming, pick one thing:

- Open a separate savings account and set up an automatic transfer on payday
- List every debt you have with its interest rate
- Track your spending for the next 30 days

One small step, done consistently, compounds over time — just like interest.
